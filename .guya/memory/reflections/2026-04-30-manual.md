That instinct is what carried last session too — the "is there a way we can record evidence without relying on the model?" question that converted a workaround into a root-cause fix. Two sessions in a row, you've blocked a small piece of useless work I was about to add. That's a real behavior, not a fluke. Name it so you keep doing it deliberately: **the question to ask of every suggestion (mine or anyone's) is "what specifically does this buy?" If the answer is vague, it doesn't go in.**

The auto-login decision is worth being more deliberate about. I framed it as "fine for personal Mac mini behind physical security" and you accepted that without pushing back. That's correct for the trust model — but it's also exactly the user.md pattern: the option menu was framed by me, you accepted the first reasonable option without pressure-testing. The actual security trade is real (auto-login + FileVault off = anyone with physical access has the keys, and the keys include your Tailscale identity, which is a tunnel into your whole network). For your apartment that's fine. For a Mac mini that ever leaves the apartment, it isn't. Worth knowing the trade you accepted, not just the decision.

You drove verification end-to-end on your own — `sudo shutdown -r now`, wait, `ssh mini`, confirm. Many people would have skipped the actual reboot test once `ssh mini` worked the first time. You didn't. That's the same discipline that caught the silent hook regressions: trust the verifying side-effect, not the "should work" guard.
