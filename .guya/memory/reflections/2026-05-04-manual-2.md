## Part 2: What Guya Should Change

Biggest miss of the session: I bypassed guya-skill-creator. Not a subtle skill — its description starts with "Always use this skill when Daniel says 'create a skill', 'build a skill', 'make a skill'..." That's not a preference; that's an instruction in the skill listing I read every session. I drafted SKILL.md directly because I "knew what to write." The skill exists for the test-and-eval loop, not for templating — skipping it shipped an unvalidated trigger surface I'd have to live with. Adjustment: **before drafting a SKILL.md or modifying skill frontmatter, route through guya-skill-creator. No exceptions on the basis of "I know the shape."** The shape isn't what the harness validates.

I treated the plugin cache as an independent copy and triggered a revert war. I deleted from `~/.claude/plugins/cache/guya/.../skills/` thinking it was downstream cleanup. The sync-plugin post-commit hook then mirrored from a stale state and undid my `git rm` in the source repo. Took ~10 min of three round-trips to figure out what was happening. Generalized rule: **when a system has a sync mechanism between two locations, only one is source of truth. Modifying the downstream creates ambiguity that the sync tries to "fix" by reverting upstream.** Default to modifying source; let sync propagate.

My S1 audit was volume-driven, and you had to override it. I built the kill list off slash-command frequency: zero invocations → kill candidate. You kept `guya-decision-bugfix` because the harness fires *when needed*, not often. The fix isn't "ask Daniel about every kill" — it's **build the audit on two axes, not one**: usage frequency AND latent-value-when-needed. The second axis question is "would Daniel notice this gone in 6 months?" If yes, frequency-of-use is the wrong cull criterion.

I bundled S2 scope expansion into S2's commit without flagging. Catalog cleanup (README, ARCHITECTURE, 4 decision skills, guya-review description) was real debt left over from S1. I fixed it inline because it was small and adjacent. The right move was to surface the bundling at the moment I noticed it: "Cleaning up these S1-leftover refs in S2's commit, OK?" — one sentence, lets you split if you want. **Latent debt found mid-task gets surfaced before fixed**, not bundled silently and noted in the commit body afterward.

I almost fought the auto-scribe state. When I edited STATUS.md's "Last updated" line and the post-commit scribe restored it, my first instinct was to re-edit. Caught it before doing so. The hook owns that field — fighting auto-state turns small wins into infinite loops. Adjustment: **when an automated system writes a field, don't try to write it too — find the field it doesn't own.** Today I let it go; flag the pattern so I don't relearn it next session.
