## Part 1: What Daniel Should Take Away

You ran the leverage move twice in this session, both times unprompted.

First: when I'd shipped the manual Step 0 workaround for auto-evidence, you didn't accept it. You asked "is there a way we can record evidence without relying on model?" That single question converted a permanent friction tax (manual step every review) into a root-cause fix plus a defense layer that catches the entire class of bug. The workaround would have stuck around for months — comfortable, "responsible," wrong. You didn't let it.

Second: when I offered to build the smoke test, you said "write it." Three syllables, no hedging, no exploration. That's convergence under direct instruction — exactly the pattern you've been trying to build, and it ships work fast.

You also predicted the Step 0 timing failure ("you stage after recording → the gate blocks") before it happened, and chose to let it happen rather than warn me. That's a defensible call — real-world test of whether I'd self-discover — but worth naming: you held back useful information and accepted the cost. Same trade-off you make when you don't pressure-test my plans before agreeing. Sometimes the cost is small. This time it cost one extra commit. Worth deciding consciously, not by default.

The parallel-session move (delegating the realpath debugging to a fresh session) is new and high-leverage. You used a separate context for the heavy debugging instead of letting it eat this one. That's operational maturity — most people would have just waited.

## Part 2: What Guya Should Change

I built a workaround before debugging the root cause. When auto-evidence didn't fire, my first instinct was Step 0 in SKILL.md — manual scaffold the model has to remember. It looks responsible and ships fast, but it costs forever. The right first move was to ask "is the hook even firing?" — five minutes of diagnostic instead of a permanent scar. You had to push me onto the right path with the "without relying on model" question. Next time auto-anything doesn't work: instrument first, scaffold second.

I almost shipped the smoke test without proving it catches the bug. Wrote it, ran it, all green, was about to commit. Only then thought to revert `realpathSync` and confirm the test fires. A passing test that doesn't fail when the bug returns is confirmation bias dressed as confidence. Test the test, every time, in the first pass.

I defaulted to project scope on the deny rules. You asked me to block edits to `pre-commit-config.json`; I added them to `.claude/settings.local.json` without asking global vs project. Same pattern as the Constantia auto-vs-meaningful question from April 22 — design decision I made implicitly when I should have asked. When permissions, scope, or ownership are involved, ask first.

I kept end-of-message "want me to..." offers running through the whole session. Each one individually is fine. Together they're noise — they push decisions back at you when you've already given direction. Stopping after delivering is also a response.
